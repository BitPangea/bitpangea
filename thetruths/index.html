Structural Domain Principles

The Structural Domain preserves principles revealed by Codices that define what the world is. These principles concern existence, distinction, position, and the cartographic primitives of BitPangea.

ST-I · The Parcel

Unit · What is the world?
Principle 1 · ST-I

Existence Precedes Discovery

Parcels exist independently of whether they have been discovered, observed, mapped, claimed, or discussed.

Principle 2 · ST-I

Revelation Through Mapping

The world is revealed through cartography rather than created through issuance, allocation, or authority.

Principle 3 · ST-I

Ownership Is Secondary

Ownership may emerge later, but existence does not depend upon ownership.

Principle 4 · ST-I

The Map Must Outlast Its Inhabitants

Cartography should endure beyond individual participants, institutions, and generations.

Principle 5 · ST-I

Identity Persists

A Parcel remains itself regardless of ownership, stewardship, settlement, or historical change.

ST-II · The Boundary

Distinction · What is the world?
Principle 1 · ST-II

Distinction Enables Plurality

Multiple Parcels can only exist if distinction exists between them.

Principle 2 · ST-II

Boundaries Precede Relationships

Distinction must exist before relationships can exist.

Principle 3 · ST-II

A Boundary Is Not A Claim

Distinction does not imply ownership.

Principle 4 · ST-II

A Boundary Belongs To The World

Boundaries are properties of BitPangea itself rather than individual Parcels.

Principle 5 · ST-II

Representation Is Not Definition

A map may represent a Boundary, but the Boundary exists independently of representation.

ST-III · The Coordinate

Location · What is the world?
Principle 1 · ST-III

Position Precedes Description

A Parcel possesses position because it exists within BitPangea. A Coordinate describes that position. It does not create it.

Principle 2 · ST-III

Coordinates Make Position Referable

Position may exist without being known, named, mapped, or described. Coordinates allow position to be referenced within the cartographic record.

Principle 3 · ST-III

A Coordinate Is Not A Claim

A Coordinate describes where a Parcel is. It does not determine who may claim, steward, govern, inhabit, or use it.

Principle 4 · ST-III

Coordinates Belong To The Map

Coordinates are part of the cartographic structure of BitPangea and do not belong to any person, institution, community, or Parcel owner.

Principle 5 · ST-III

Representation Is Not Implementation

A Coordinate may be expressed through systems, but such systems represent Coordinates. They do not define the constitutional concept itself.

ST-IV · The World

Totality · What is the world?
Principle 1 · ST-IV

Structural Concepts Exist Within The World

Parcels, Boundaries, Coordinates, and all future Structural concepts exist within The World. They do not create it.

Principle 2 · ST-IV

Structural Concepts Derive Meaning Through The World

No Structural concept can be fully understood in isolation. Each derives meaning through participation in the singular whole.

Principle 3 · ST-IV

Structural Discovery Does Not Create The World

Discovery reveals what already exists within The World. Discovery is an act of recognition rather than creation.

Principle 4 · ST-IV

Structural Discovery Does Not Expand The World

The World remains fixed regardless of future inquiry. Discovery may increase knowledge of The World, but it does not enlarge it.

Principle 5 · ST-IV

The World Contains All Structural Concepts

All present and future Structural concepts participate within the same cartographic whole.

Principle 6 · ST-IV

The World Precedes Structural Inquiry

The World exists prior to its observation, description, mapping, or codification. Structural inquiry begins within a world that already exists.

Relational Domain Principles

The Relational Domain preserves principles revealed by Codices that define how things relate. These principles concern arrangement, connection, proximity, and relation among structural entities.

RL-I · The Adjacency

Relation of Proximity · How do things relate?
Principle 1 · RL-I

Adjacency Requires Distinction

Relationship depends upon distinction.

Principle 2 · RL-I

Adjacency Requires Position

Relationship depends upon location.

Principle 3 · RL-I

Adjacency Is Not Ownership

Proximity does not establish rights, claims, or authority.

Principle 4 · RL-I

Adjacency Belongs To The World

Relationships are properties of the world's structure.

Principle 5 · RL-I

Relationship Precedes Meaning

Cartographic relationships exist before social, economic, or civilizational meaning emerges.

RL-II · The Contiguity

Condition of Connection · How do things relate?
Principle 1 · RL-II

Contiguity Requires Adjacency

Connection depends upon one or more Adjacencies between Parcels.

Principle 2 · RL-II

Connection Emerges Through Relation

Parcels become connected through cartographic relationships rather than ownership, governance, settlement, commerce, or activity.

Principle 3 · RL-II

Contiguity Preserves Distinction

Connection does not erase individuality. Distinct Parcels remain distinct even within a Contiguity.

Principle 4 · RL-II

A Contiguity Is Not A Claim

Connection does not establish ownership, stewardship, authority, jurisdiction, access, or control.

Principle 5 · RL-II

The World Remains Whole

BitPangea itself constitutes a single Contiguity connected through an unbroken path of Adjacencies.

Principle 6 · RL-II

No Parcel Exists In Cartographic Isolation

Every Parcel participates in the greater structure of BitPangea and exists within the world's Contiguity.
