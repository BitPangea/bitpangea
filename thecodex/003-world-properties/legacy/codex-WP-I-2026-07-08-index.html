The Extent

The First World Property

Codex WP-I opens the World Properties Domain by establishing Extent as the first constitutional property of The World. Following the completion of the Structural and Relational Domains, BitPangea now begins describing what is true about the World itself.

Domain Assignment

Codex WP-I belongs to the World Properties Domain (WP).

The World Properties Domain asks: What properties does the World possess? It preserves Codices that describe properties of The World rather than the primitives composing it or the pairwise relationships between its Parcels.

The World Properties Domain examines properties possessed by the World itself. Some properties are intrinsic to the World, while others become constitutionally recognizable through the relational grammar established by the Relational Domain. The purpose of this Domain is to describe those properties rather than to create or assemble them.

Constitutional Transition

The Structural Domain culminated in Codex ST-IV — The World, recognizing The World as the singular, continuous cartographic whole within BitPangea.

The Relational Domain then established the first-order grammar by which Parcels may be understood in relation to one another through Adjacency, Contiguity, Distance, and Direction.

Codex WP-I begins the next constitutional inquiry. The Codex now asks not what exists, nor how Parcels relate, but what properties are possessed by the World that has already been declared and structurally recognized.

Spatial Properties

The first category within the World Properties Domain is Spatial Properties.

Spatial Properties describe the cartographic character of The World as a spatial whole. They begin with the broadest property of the World and proceed toward increasingly localized properties recognized within its scope.

WP-I · Extent

The spatial scope of the World.

WP-II · Region

A large-scale spatial property recognized within the World.

WP-III · Cluster

A spatial property of concentration or grouping within the World.

WP-IV · Locality

A localized spatial property recognized within the World.

Future categories of World Properties may include physical, topological, environmental, temporal, or other properties revealed through later constitutional inquiry.

The Definition

The existence of The World makes property inquiry possible.

An Extent exists not to establish ownership, governance, jurisdiction, settlement, value, accessibility, measurement system, or cartographic implementation, but to describe the spatial scope possessed by The World.

Extent is the first property stated about The World because it concerns the World as a whole before examining regions, clusters, localities, or other spatial properties within it.

Formal Definition

An Extent is the cartographic property describing the spatial scope of the World within BitPangea.

Extent is a property of The World rather than a property of any individual Parcel, Boundary, Coordinate, relation, region, cluster, locality, inhabitant, claim, or institution.

This Codex establishes that The World possesses spatial scope. It does not establish the method by which that scope is measured, represented, mapped, or implemented.

Immutable Properties

Every Extent possesses the following immutable properties:

World-Level Property

Extent belongs to The World as a whole rather than to any individual Parcel or lesser spatial structure.

Spatial Scope

Extent describes the cartographic scope within which all spatial properties of The World are recognized.

Priority

Extent is recognized before Region, Cluster, Locality, or other localized spatial properties.

Dependence

Extent depends upon the prior constitutional recognition of The World and the relational grammar through which spatial properties become recognizable.

Permanence

Inquiry, mapping, recognition, or codification does not create, expand, contract, or alter the Extent of The World.

Neutrality

Extent does not assign ownership, jurisdiction, access, value, settlement, governance, movement, purpose, or civilizational meaning. It merely describes spatial scope.

What An Extent Is

  • A cartographic property
  • A property of The World
  • The spatial scope of The World
  • The first Spatial Property within the World Properties Domain
  • The first constitutional statement made about The World after its structural recognition

What An Extent Is Not

  • A Parcel
  • A Boundary
  • A Coordinate
  • A relation between Parcels
  • A Region
  • A Cluster
  • A Locality
  • A claim, territory, settlement, or government
  • A cartographic implementation or measurement system

Principles Established by Codex WP-I

1. The World Possesses Properties

The World is not merely a structural whole. It possesses properties that may be constitutionally described without being created or assembled by the Codex.

2. Properties Are Described, Not Created

The World Properties Domain describes properties possessed by The World. Inquiry, recognition, mapping, or codification does not create those properties.

3. Extent Precedes Localized Spatial Properties

The spatial scope of The World is recognized before Regions, Clusters, Localities, or other localized spatial properties are constitutionally described.

4. Relational Grammar Enables Recognition

Some World Properties become constitutionally recognizable through the relational grammar established by Adjacency, Contiguity, Distance, and Direction.

5. The World Is Revealed Rather Than Assembled

World Properties are examined as properties of the already-declared World, not as products assembled upward from Parcels or other constituent parts.

6. Spatial Scope Does Not Imply Civilizational Meaning

Extent establishes no ownership, administration, settlement, governance, access, value, purpose, movement, or use.

Scope

This Codex acknowledges the existence and necessity of Extent as the first Spatial Property within the World Properties Domain.

It establishes that The World possesses spatial scope. It does not determine the specific measurement, geometry, surface character, implementation, or representational method by which that scope may later be described.

It does not establish:

  • The nature of the Surface
  • The character of the Limit
  • The geometry of the World's outer edge
  • The precise method by which spatial scope is measured
  • The number, boundaries, or character of Regions
  • The criteria by which Clusters or Localities are recognized
  • Ownership, claims, governance, commerce, settlement, or civilizational meaning
  • Cartographic implementation

These questions remain subjects for future inquiry.

Constitutional Sequence

Codex WP-I marks the transition from structural and relational inquiry into property inquiry.

The Structural Domain asks what exists. The Relational Domain asks how Parcels relate. The World Properties Domain asks what properties the World possesses.

Extent is therefore not an extension of the Structural Domain and not a continuation of the Relational Domain. It is the first property of The World to be constitutionally described after The World has been declared, structurally recognized, and relationally understood.

Cartographic Doctrine The World is declared. Relational grammar reveals how Parcels relate. World Properties describe what the World possesses.
